There were chapel gates to the right and left, staircases
and raised choirs, and the pilgrim soon sees that this great
house of God is an assemblage of differing rites and
modes of worship, and a compromise of their claims, and
that they all find room in the one space for their own
special services. In the centre of the church, in the great
circular hall, one chapel stands-a temple by itself; it is the
true Chapel of the Sepulchre, which belongs in common
to the Eastern and Latin Churches, and in which all the
ancient sects hold their worship. The newer faiths alone
are excluded-the Protestants and those of similar tenets.

Ere we reached the Chapel of the Sepulchre the whole
procession knelt by the great square "stone of anointing,"
with its heavy candelabra round, and lying flat on the
ground we all kissed it. It is the stone on which the body
of Christ lay when anointed by Nicodemus. As the
Scripture tells: "After this Joseph of Arimathtea, being
a disciple of Jesus, but secretly, for fear of the Jews, besought
Pilate that he might take away the body of Jesus: and
Pilate gave him leave. He came therefore, and took the
body of Jesus. And there came also N icodemus, which at
the first came to Jesus by night, and brought a mixture of
myrrh and aloes, about an hundred pounds. Then took
they the body of Jesus, and wound it in linen clothes with
the spices, as the manner of the Jews is to bury."
