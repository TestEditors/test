<br/>
The train was a quick one, so we barely caught a glimpse<br/>
of many interesting places as we sped by. At first the<br/>
way lies along a low ridge, which separates the two great<br/>
marshy lakes of Mareotis on the right and Aboukir on<br/>
the left from one another. The broad surface of these<br/>
waters was covered with water-fowl of all descriptions,.<br/>
and on the yellow sandbanks stood dark herons with<br/>
outstretched necks.<br/>
<br/>
After a while, the water and marshes of the northern<br/>
Delta were left behind, and gave place to richly cultivated<br/>
land. Everywhere there was tillage, broad <span style="color: #ff0000;">cornfiedis</span>, and<br/>
downright forests of cotton plants, deep canals and high<br/>
dykes, here and there groups of slender young palms,<br/>
dusky gardens, and ruined villages, brown and clay-built,<br/>
with their lofty minarets. Such is the uniform type of the<br/>
cultivated part of Lower Egypt.<br/>
